# Understanding Electromagnetism

One of the most elegant aspects of physics is the deep connection between electricity and magnetism. When $\mathbf{E}$ (electric) and $\mathbf{B}$ (magnetic) fields interact, they create a self-sustaining dance of energy that forms the basis of electromagnetic waves.

<h2 id="maxwell">Maxwell's Equations</h2>

The relationship between electric and magnetic fields is captured in Maxwell's equations:

$$
\begin{align*}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} &= \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \\
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0 \\
\nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \\
\nabla \times \mathbf{B} &= \mu_0\left(\mathbf{J} + \varepsilon_0\frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right)
\end{align*}
$$

These equations tell us something remarkable: a changing electric field creates a magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field creates an electric field.

The Lorentz force $\mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ describes how charged particles respond to both electric and magnetic fields. When a charge $q$ moves with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ through these fields, it experiences a combined force.

## Code Implementation

Here's a simple Python function to calculate the magnitude of the Lorentz force for a particle moving perpendicular to a magnetic field:

```python
def lorentz_force(q, v, B):
    """
    Calculate the magnitude of the Lorentz force for perpendicular motion

    Parameters:
    q (float): Charge in Coulombs
    v (float): Velocity in m/s
    B (float): Magnetic field strength in Tesla

    Returns:
    float: Force in Newtons
    """
    return abs(q * v * B)

# Example: electron in a 1 Tesla field
electron_charge = -1.602e-19  # Coulombs
velocity = 1e6               # 1 million m/s
magnetic_field = 1.0        # Tesla

force = lorentz_force(electron_charge, velocity, magnetic_field)
print(f"Force magnitude: {force:.2e} N")
```

## Conclusion

The interplay between electric and magnetic fields, mathematically described by Maxwell's equations where $\varepsilon_0$ is the vacuum permittivity and $\mu_0$ is the vacuum permeability, forms the foundation of many modern technologies, from electric generators to particle accelerators.

The energy density in electromagnetic fields is given by:

$$
u = \frac{1}{2}\left(\varepsilon_0E^2 + \frac{B^2}{\mu_0}\right)
$$

This equation shows how both electric and magnetic fields contribute to the total electromagnetic energy density in space.
